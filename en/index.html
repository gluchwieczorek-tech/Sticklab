The Stick Lab Manifesto

Ten principles for buying a junior stick.

Every game is practice.

Junior hockey is for developing players, not winning matches. Don't optimize for game day — optimize for what helps your kid grow as a player.

USA Hockey ADM: "There is no such thing as an elite 12-year-old."
Hockey Canada: No position specialization until U13.
Sweden, Norway: No standings or stats <U13.
IIHF: "Their goal is not to develop into a hockey player, but to have fun."

Equipment is supplementary, not central.

Hours on ice always beat the latest stick. No federation prescribes specific brands or models — they focus on player development.

Get the basics right; the rest is education.

Length, flex, and shooting hand are critical. Kick point, curve, and lie are useful to understand but rarely make a meaningful difference. Knowing them helps you make conscious changes later.

Round flex DOWN, never up.

Body weight in kg × 1.1, then round down to the nearest available flex. Going over is the most common mistake. Most players use sticks that are too stiff.

Match the stick to the budget.

Top tier sticks make sense if you can afford them. If you can't, there is no evidence they will help your child in any meaningful way.

"Decent mid-tier" beats both extremes.

Wood is heavier (effects on technique are nuanced — some pros train heavy on purpose). Flagships have no proven advantage for juniors. Mid-tier composite, ideally previous generation, is the sweet spot.

The pros disagree with the marketing.

NHL players routinely use stick models that are 5-10 years old. Manufacturers sometimes paint old sticks to look new. If pros prioritize familiarity over newness, why shouldn't your kid?

Documented at HockeyStickMan and GearGeek (NHL equipment database, 855 active players).

Junior shafts are physically different.

Smaller circumference for smaller hands. A cut-down senior stick will not give the same hand-fit as a true junior model.

IIHF Learn to Play: "Lighter sticks for smaller players."
Polish hockey tradition (PZHL-aligned): "Trzonek odpowiednio cienki" (shaft must be appropriately thin).

Junior performance data is largely missing.

Almost all reviews and tutorials test senior sticks. Manufacturer claims about junior versions ("same technology") are not independently verified. Be skeptical of premium pricing for junior models.

Personal preference beats every schema.

Every player has their own preferences worth discovering. NHL pros use 55, 80, 120 flex despite formulas. Generic recommendations get you started; experimentation finds the right stick.

For first sticks, start with minimal curve.

Aggressive curves (P28, W28) are for skilled players who already know what they like. Beginners benefit from minimal or mid curves for learning fundamentals — backhand, passing, basic shooting.

PZHL-aligned tradition: "Krzywizna jak najmniejsza" (curve as minimal as possible) for kids.

📏 Sizing

How long should the stick be?

Standard practice in European/Nordic youth hockey

✅ With skates off: stick reaches roughly between the eyebrows.
✅ With skates on: stick reaches roughly the lips/mouth.

The skate boost (~3-4 cm) is what makes the difference between the two. Both measurements describe the same stick — just measured in different conditions.

What matters most: watch how your kid holds it on the ice. If they're hunched over too much, it's too short. If they're standing too upright and the blade is barely on the ice, it's too long.

Cutting a stick raises the flex. Each inch (~2.5 cm) cut off adds roughly 3-5 to the flex number. If you cut 5 cm off a 40 flex, you're now playing closer to 50 flex.

And the opposite — extensions lower the flex slightly by giving you more leverage. Add 4-6 inch (~10-15 cm) and your 40 flex becomes effectively closer to 35. See the "Stick extensions" section below.

🔧 Stick extensions

Don't buy a new stick. Extend the old one.

Confirmed Kids grow 4-7 cm per year. A stick that fits perfectly today is too short in 6-12 months. Most parents buy a new stick when this happens. You don't have to.

Stick extensions (also called "end plugs" or "butt extensions") add 4-6 inches (~10-15 cm) to the top of the shaft. They're glued or epoxied in place. Two main types:

1. Wood extensions — cheapest, ~50-100 NOK. Add some weight at the top (which actually helps with stick balance for kids who feel their stick is "tip-heavy"). Available at most hockey shops.

2. Composite/foam extensions — light, branded by Bauer/CCM/Warrior, ~80-150 NOK. Match the stick aesthetically.

3. 🖨️ 3D-printed extensions — free if you have access to a printer (school, library, makerspace, friend). Plenty of free designs:

→ Search "hockey stick extension" on Thingiverse or Printables.com — multiple free designs for standard senior/junior shaft dimensions. Most print in PLA or PETG in 3-6 hours.

Installation: remove the existing butt end (often a small plastic plug — pry it out), test-fit the extension, then secure with hot glue or 2-component epoxy. Re-tape the top. Whole job takes 15-20 minutes.

Effect on flex: a longer stick with the same blade is slightly whippier — you have more leverage. Adding 5 cm of extension softens the effective flex by roughly 3-5 points. Usually a good thing for growing kids who haven't fully filled out the stick yet.

When NOT to extend: if the stick is already cracked or damaged, replace it. If the kid has clearly outgrown the flex (e.g., went from 30 kg to 45 kg over the season), they need a stiffer stick anyway, not just a longer one.

✂️ Cutting sticks

Cutting a composite stick: two things nobody tells you.

Confirmed Sticks rarely come in exactly the right length, so cutting is normal. But two things matter that most parents never hear about — one affects the stick, one affects you.

1. Cutting makes the stick stiffer. The material doesn't change, but a shorter shaft gives less leverage — so it takes more force to bend it. Rule of thumb: roughly +3–5 flex per inch (2.5 cm) you cut off. And the effect is bigger on junior sticks, because each centimeter is a larger share of a short shaft. Cut 5 cm off a junior 40-flex and your kid is effectively playing a ~48 — which quietly breaks the "flex ≈ weight × 1.1" rule this whole site is built on. If you know you'll cut a lot, buy one flex step softer. (Extensions work the other way: adding length makes the stick softer — see the card above.)

Health 2. Carbon fiber dust is a real lung hazard. Composite sticks are carbon fiber in epoxy resin. Sawing releases a fine, respirable dust that's documented as harmful to breathe (this is standard occupational-safety territory — OSHA covers composite machining). It's also nasty for skin and eyes: the fibers are essentially microscopic splinters. This is not "wood shop dust". Treat it seriously.

If you cut at home: do it outdoors or in a well-ventilated space, wear a dust mask (P2/FFP2 or better) and glasses, long sleeves if you're sensitive. Wrap a layer of tape around the cut line first — it reduces splintering and gives a clean edge. Use a fine-tooth hacksaw (high tooth count), not a coarse wood saw — coarse blades chip and delaminate the carbon layers. Cut with light, even strokes. Afterwards, wipe dust up with a damp cloth (don't blow it around), lightly sand the edge smooth, and re-tape the top.

The easy answer: let the shop do it. Almost every hockey shop cuts sticks for free or nearly free when you buy — they have proper saws and it takes them 30 seconds. Mark the length at home first (kid in skates, stick vertical, mark at lips — see the sizing card), so you're not guessing in the store.

Cut too much? Don't panic — that's exactly what extensions are for (previous card).

🎯 Kick point

What is a kick point?

When you take a shot, the stick bends and snaps back — that's what gives your shot its power. The "kick point" is where on the shaft the bend happens. It's not a real point on the stick — it's a feature of how the stick is engineered to flex.

Why it matters: a low-kick stick loads (bends) closer to the blade, snapping back faster — great for quick wrist shots in tight spaces. A mid-kick stick loads in the middle, takes longer to bend but stores more energy — better for slap shots from the point. Hybrid splits the difference.

Manifesto note For a developing junior, this difference is real but smaller than marketing suggests. A kid can score with any kick point — the kick point shapes how they shoot, not whether they can. Knowing the differences helps you make conscious changes later (e.g. "let's try a low kick next year and compare"). Many NHL snipers use mid-kick sticks (Auston Matthews, Steven Stamkos) — there's no rule.

🌀 Curves

What is a blade curve?

A blade curve is the shape of the bend built into the blade. A flat blade can shoot, but a curved one gives you better puck control, more lift on shots, and — critically — different feel for different play styles. The curve is defined by where the bend is along the blade and how deep it is.

📜 A bit of history

For the first 130 years of hockey, blades were flat. The curve appeared by accident in the late 1950s. The dispute over who really invented it is unsettled — Andy Bathgate (NY Rangers) claimed he'd been bending blades since his youth in 1940s Winnipeg, while Stan Mikita (Chicago Blackhawks) credits his own accidental discovery: a broken blade in practice that made the puck dance unpredictably. Mikita and his teammate Bobby Hull began bending blades on purpose — jamming them in bench doors and pouring hot water on them.

By the mid-1960s Hull's "banana blade" had curves up to 3 inches deep. Goalies (most still without masks) hated it — pucks moved like knuckleballs and goalie injuries surged. The NHL responded with rules: in 1967 the league began limiting curves, settling at 1/2 inch in 1970.

Today the limit is 19 mm (3/4 inch) in both NHL (since 2005-06) and IIHF rules. The IIHF rule applies internationally, including Norwegian, Polish, and Swedish hockey at competitive levels. Practically: every retail junior stick is well within legal limits — you only need to think about this if someone modifies blades by heat-bending (rare these days since composite blades replaced wood).

Polish hockey tradition For young or beginner kids, "krzywizna jak najmniejsza" — the most minimal curve possible. Aggressive toe curves (P28) are designed for skilled players who know what they want. Beginners benefit from balanced mid curves to learn fundamentals like backhand and passing.

A small note on CCM "+1/4" variants

On hockeystore.no's CCM filter you'll see options like P28+1/4, P29+1/4. These aren't different curves — they're the same shape with the blade made taller (more vertical surface, same curve depth). The idea is more area for batting pucks out of the air, blocking shots, and corralling bouncing pucks. Independent measurements (ABSHockey) suggest the actual height difference is small — closer to marketing nuance than dramatic change. Skip these for juniors — stick with base P28 / P29 until your kid has played 1-2 seasons and asks specifically. Bauer/Warrior/True don't typically offer +1/4 variants in junior models.

🤲 Hand selection

Left or right shot?

No universal "correct" answer

In a hockey grip, the bottom hand defines the shot side. Left hand at bottom = left-handed shot.

Conventional wisdom (varies by region):

🇺🇸🇨🇦 North America: dominant hand on top (for stickhandling control). Right-hand dominant kids → left-handed shot.
🇪🇺 Parts of Europe: dominant hand on bottom (for shot power). Right-hand dominant → right-handed shot.

NHL data: ~70% of NHL players shoot left, even though only ~10% of the general population is left-handed. The relationship between handedness and shooting side is not 1:1.

Best practical method: find an old straight-blade wood stick (loaner pile, used market, family attic). Let your kid grip it both ways. Watch what feels natural for stickhandling and receiving passes. Once they've played for ~6 months on one side, don't switch.

⚠️ Junior data gap

An honest caveat about everything you read online

Limited data

Almost every hockey stick review, weight test, kick-point comparison, and durability assessment online tests senior sticks tested by adult reviewers.

Manufacturer pages claim junior versions use "the same technology" — Nanolite Shield, Sigmatex carbon, Twitch Taper, and so on. These claims are not independently verified. Junior weight figures are rarely published. Kick-point feel for kids is unstudied.

What we know for certain:

✅ Junior sticks have smaller shaft circumference (smaller hands).
✅ Junior sticks come in lower flex options (20, 30, 40, 50).
✅ Junior shaft length is shorter.

What's marketing claim only:

⚠️ Junior version of flagship = same performance as senior flagship.
⚠️ Junior weight figures match marketing photos.
⚠️ Junior kick point feels exactly like senior kick point.

Practical implication: the premium for a junior flagship is largely a marketing premium. Mid-tier and previous-generation junior models often deliver similar real-world value at a fraction of the cost.

🎀 Taping

Taping the stick: blade, knob, grip — what actually matters.

Every player tapes a bit differently — this is the basic taping we recommend starting with. Video: «How To Tape A Hockey Stick» by The Hockey Shop (YouTube) — credit to the creator.

Standard practice Tape isn't decoration. On the blade it protects the composite from chips and moisture, and gives the puck something to grip. On the butt end it's a safety feature. Total cost: ~40 NOK per roll, one roll lasts a young player months.

Blade — how: tape from heel to toe, so each wrap overlaps the previous one in the direction of the ice — the tape lies flat instead of catching. Overlap about half the tape width, keep even tension, no wrinkles (wrinkles under the puck = wobbly passes). Tear the tape off at the top edge of the blade, never the bottom — a tail on the bottom peels off with the first stride. Smooth everything down with your hand or rub it with an old puck.

White or black? For kids learning the game: white. They can see the puck against the blade, which genuinely helps stickhandling development. Black tape "hides" the puck from goalies — a marginal advantage that matters at higher levels, not at 10 years old. Bonus: white tape stains glove palms less. Skip the patterned/novelty tapes on the blade — they're cloth quality lottery.

Wax (kølle­voks): a thin layer of stick wax over the blade tape repels water and snow, so the tape lasts longer and ice doesn't build up. Useful, not mandatory — the budget version is rubbing the taped blade with an old puck. Never use clear shin-pad tape on a blade.

Knob (butt end) — this one is safety: a taped knob keeps the stick from sliding through the glove, makes it possible to pick up off the ice with gloves on, and — the part that matters for kids — a bare shaft end is a blunt pipe in a fall or against a face. Many youth leagues treat an uncovered butt end as illegal equipment. Size it for the kid's hand: a few wraps is enough, a goalie-sized ball interferes with the glove.

Shaft grip: tape only the top ~1.5 glove-lengths — the lower hand must slide freely, so never tape the whole shaft (it also adds pointless weight to that expensive light stick). A "candy cane" spiral or a thin under-tape ridge is fine if the kid likes the feel; skip elaborate finger-groove jobs for young players, they lock the hand in one spot.

When to re-tape: when the bottom edge of the blade tape frays through — for a young player that's every few sessions, not every day. Fresh tape before a game is a nice ritual, not a requirement. Keep a roll in the bag.

📋 Summary

What's actually important — a priority guide

You've read a lot. Here's how to think about all of it when you actually make a decision. Three buckets, in order of how much they affect your kid's development.

🟢 ESSENTIAL — get these right

These are non-negotiable. Get any of these wrong and your kid is fighting their equipment instead of learning the game.

✅ Length — between eyebrows (no skates) / lips (on skates). Too short = hunched over and back pain. Too long = no puck control.
✅ Flex — weight × 1.1, round DOWN. Too stiff = can't load the stick = bad shooting technique forming. Better to err on softer side.
✅ Hand — left or right shot. Test with a broomstick or straight wood stick at home before buying. Wrong hand = wasted money and frustration.

🟡 IMPORTANT — worth thinking about

These shape how your kid plays. They matter, but they're preferences more than fundamentals. You can usually figure out what works through trial.

🔸 Curve family — toe (P28), mid (P29/P92), heel (P88). Beginners: stay with mid. Don't worry about cross-brand naming.
🔸 Kick point — low (snipers), mid (slap-shots), hybrid (everyone else). Less critical than marketing makes it out to be.
🔸 Tier — flagship vs mid-tier vs budget. For kids under 13, mid-tier (or last-gen flagship on sale) is the sweet spot. No proven advantage of pro sticks for juniors.
🔸 Brand — pick what's available locally. Curves are equivalent across brands. Brand loyalty is mostly marketing.

⚪ NICE TO KNOW — don't stress

You can read about these to understand the marketing, but they shouldn't drive your decision.

⚙️ Curve variants like P28+1/4 — taller blade, same curve. Skip for juniors.
⚙️ Exact stick weight in grams — junior weights aren't independently verified anyway, and a few grams won't change anything for a developing player.
⚙️ "Latest tech" / "new this season" — NHL pros use 5-10 year old sticks. Last-gen on clearance is fine.
⚙️ Specific blade construction (foam core, carbon weave) — hard to even verify, harder to feel.
⚙️ Aesthetics — color, graphics, raised vs flat grip. Whatever your kid likes is fine.

🎯 The single best decision-making rule

Match the stick to the budget, not the budget to the stick.

If you can spend 800 NOK, buy the best 800 NOK stick that fits length+flex+hand. If you can spend 3000 NOK, buy the best 3000 NOK stick that fits. Either way, your kid will improve. The difference between a 800 NOK and a 3000 NOK stick is much smaller than the difference between practicing twice a week and three times a week.

Equipment is supplementary, not central. The kid is the player, not the stick.
